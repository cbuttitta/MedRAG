The Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension (DASH) diet is proven to help lower blood pressure and is recommended by the National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute. The DASH diet is rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and low-fat dairy products, and low in saturated fat, total fat, and sodium. Its effects on blood pressure are sometimes seen within a few weeks. The DASH diet also emphasizes foods higher in potassium, calcium, and magnesium, which all contribute to blood pressure regulation.
Sodium intake has a direct impact on blood pressure in most people with hypertension. Limiting sodium to no more than 2,300 mg per day is recommended, and reducing to 1,500 mg per day is an even more effective goal for people with high blood pressure. The primary sources of sodium in the American diet are packaged and processed foods, restaurant meals, bread, and canned goods — not the salt shaker at the table. Reading food labels to identify sodium content is an essential skill for blood pressure management.
Getting at least 150 minutes of moderate to vigorous aerobic exercise per week is a key lifestyle recommendation for people with hypertension. Regular aerobic exercise such as brisk walking, jogging, cycling, or swimming strengthens the heart, reduces arterial stiffness, and can lower systolic blood pressure by 4 to 9 mmHg. Exercise benefits accumulate over time and are most effective when combined with dietary changes and weight management.
Maintaining a healthy weight significantly reduces blood pressure. Even losing 5 to 10 pounds can measurably lower blood pressure in people who are overweight. Extra body weight increases the workload on the heart and raises blood pressure by requiring more cardiac output to supply tissues. Weight loss through a combination of caloric reduction and regular physical activity is one of the most effective non-pharmacological treatments for hypertension.
Limiting alcohol consumption is recommended for people with high blood pressure. Drinking too much alcohol can raise blood pressure and reduce the effectiveness of blood pressure medications. Men should limit alcohol to no more than 2 drinks per day and women to no more than 1 drink per day. A standard drink is defined as 12 ounces of regular beer, 5 ounces of wine, or 1.5 ounces of distilled spirits.
Managing stress is an important component of blood pressure control. Chronic stress can raise blood pressure by triggering the release of hormones that cause the heart to beat faster and blood vessels to narrow. Effective stress management strategies include regular physical activity, relaxation techniques such as deep breathing and progressive muscle relaxation, adequate sleep, and reducing sources of chronic stress where possible. Short-term spikes in blood pressure from stress may contribute to long-term hypertension in vulnerable individuals.
Low-fat dairy products appear to be especially beneficial for lowering systolic blood pressure due to their calcium and potassium content. Reducing saturated fat to no more than 6 percent of daily calories and total fat to 27 percent of daily calories is recommended as part of a heart-healthy diet for blood pressure management. Increasing potassium intake through foods such as bananas, potatoes, beans, and leafy greens helps counteract the blood-pressure-raising effects of sodium.
Quitting smoking lowers blood pressure and dramatically reduces overall cardiovascular risk. Each cigarette causes a temporary but sharp increase in blood pressure and heart rate. Long-term smoking damages artery walls, promotes atherosclerosis, and compounds the cardiovascular harm of high blood pressure. The combination of hypertension and smoking creates a multiplicative increase in heart attack and stroke risk.
