The main treatments for high cholesterol are heart-healthy lifestyle changes and medicines. Heart-healthy lifestyle changes that can help lower or control cholesterol include heart-healthy eating, aiming for a healthy weight, getting regular physical activity, managing stress, quitting smoking, and getting enough good-quality sleep. For some people, making lifestyle changes alone is not enough to lower cholesterol sufficiently, and medication may also be needed.
A heart-healthy eating plan limits the amount of saturated and trans fats consumed. Saturated fats are found in red meats, full-fat dairy products, butter, and many baked and processed foods. Trans fats may be found in some margarines, fried foods, and packaged snack foods. Replacing these with unsaturated fats — found in avocados, olive oil, nuts, and fatty fish — helps lower LDL cholesterol and improve the overall lipid profile.
Eating more dietary fiber is one of the most effective dietary strategies for lowering LDL cholesterol. Soluble fiber, found in oats, oat bran, beans, lentils, apples, and barley, binds to cholesterol in the digestive system and removes it from the body before it can be absorbed. Aiming for at least 25 to 38 grams of total fiber per day, with emphasis on soluble fiber sources, is recommended for people with elevated LDL cholesterol.
Getting regular physical activity is an important component of cholesterol management. Studies have shown that aerobic exercise can lower LDL cholesterol and triglycerides and raise HDL cholesterol. At least 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity such as brisk walking, cycling, or swimming is recommended. Even modest increases in daily movement produce meaningful improvements in the lipid profile over time.
To raise HDL (good) cholesterol, eating good fats instead of bad fats is important. This means limiting saturated fats from full-fat milk, cheese, high-fat meats, and foods made with butter, lard, or shortening, and avoiding trans fats. Instead, eating unsaturated fats found in avocado, olive oil, and nuts is recommended. Limiting refined carbohydrates and added sugars also helps raise HDL and lower triglycerides.
Quitting smoking can raise HDL cholesterol. Since HDL helps remove LDL cholesterol from arteries, having more HDL can help lower LDL cholesterol levels. Smoking also directly damages blood vessels, which compounds the cardiovascular risk associated with high LDL. Smoking cessation is one of the most impactful lifestyle interventions for improving overall cardiovascular health.
Getting 7 to 9 hours of good-quality sleep every night is associated with lower risk of high LDL cholesterol and high total cholesterol. Poor sleep disrupts hormonal regulation, increases inflammation, and can negatively affect lipid metabolism. Sleep hygiene practices such as maintaining a consistent sleep schedule, limiting screen time before bed, and avoiding caffeine in the afternoon support both sleep quality and cardiovascular health.
Maintaining a healthy weight is an important component of cholesterol management. If you are overweight, losing even a modest amount of weight — 5 to 10 percent of body weight — can help lower LDL cholesterol and raise HDL cholesterol. This is especially important for people with metabolic syndrome, a cluster of conditions including abdominal obesity, high triglycerides, low HDL, high blood pressure, and elevated fasting glucose that together raise the risk of heart disease and diabetes.
