The most important lifestyle approach to treating and managing type 2 diabetes is being active and eating healthy foods. Learning diabetes management skills will help you live well with the disease. These skills help prevent health problems and reduce the need for medical care. Key skills include knowing what, when, and how much to eat; how to safely increase physical activity and control weight; how to recognize and treat low and high blood sugar; and how to handle sick days.
Type 2 diabetes can be managed and sometimes improved through consistent lifestyle changes. You should have good control of your blood sugar. If your blood sugar is not controlled, serious problems called complications can happen to your body over time, affecting the eyes, kidneys, nerves, heart, and feet. The goal of all diabetes management — whether through diet, exercise, or medication — is to keep blood glucose levels as close to the target range as possible.
Blood pressure should be checked at least once a year in people with diabetes. The blood pressure goal for most people with diabetes is lower than 130/80 mm Hg. Cholesterol and triglyceride levels should also be checked once a year. Managing both blood pressure and cholesterol is critical because people with type 2 diabetes have a significantly higher risk of heart disease and stroke than people without diabetes.
Monitoring blood glucose levels regularly is an important part of managing diabetes. Your provider will advise how often you should check based on your treatment plan. Knowing your blood glucose patterns helps you and your provider make informed decisions about food, activity, and medication adjustments. People with diabetes should also have their HbA1c tested every 3 to 6 months to assess average blood glucose control over time.
People with type 2 diabetes should not smoke. Smoking damages blood vessels, reduces circulation, and significantly increases the risk of cardiovascular complications, kidney disease, and nerve damage — all conditions that diabetes already elevates risk for. Quitting smoking is one of the highest-impact lifestyle changes a person with diabetes can make for long-term health outcomes.
Stress management is an underrecognized component of diabetes care. Both physical and emotional stress can raise blood glucose levels by triggering the release of stress hormones such as cortisol and adrenaline. Regular relaxation practices such as deep breathing, mindfulness, or gentle yoga, combined with adequate sleep of 7 to 9 hours per night, support better glycemic control and overall wellbeing.
