Adults with type 2 diabetes should engage in at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week, spread over at least 3 days with no more than 2 consecutive days without activity. Moderate-intensity activities include brisk walking, cycling, swimming, and dancing. Even modest increases in physical activity — such as 10 to 15 extra minutes of walking per day — produce measurable improvements in blood glucose control and insulin sensitivity.
Resistance training is a critical and often overlooked component of diabetes management. Adults with type 2 diabetes should perform resistance training at least 2 days per week targeting all major muscle groups. Resistance exercise improves insulin sensitivity, increases muscle mass, and helps lower HbA1c independently of aerobic exercise. Appropriate resistance activities include weight machines, free weights, resistance bands, and body-weight exercises such as squats and push-ups.
Physical activity lowers blood glucose by increasing glucose uptake into muscle cells through insulin-independent pathways. During moderate aerobic exercise, working muscles absorb glucose from the bloodstream without requiring insulin. This effect can lower blood glucose for up to 24 hours after exercise, which is why consistent daily activity is more effective for glycemic control than occasional intense workouts.
People with diabetes should minimize prolonged sitting. Extended sedentary time is associated with worse glycemic control and increased cardiovascular risk, independent of overall physical activity levels. Interrupting sitting every 30 minutes with light activity such as standing, walking to get water, or doing gentle stretching has been shown to reduce postprandial blood glucose levels compared to continuous sitting.
Before beginning or significantly increasing a physical activity program, people with diabetes should discuss their exercise plan with their healthcare provider, particularly if they have complications such as peripheral neuropathy, autonomic neuropathy, or proliferative retinopathy, which require exercise modifications. Most people with well-controlled diabetes can safely begin a moderate walking program without special testing.
Flexibility and balance training, including yoga and tai chi, are beneficial additions to aerobic and resistance exercise for adults with diabetes, particularly older adults. These activities improve joint mobility, reduce fall risk, and have been shown to modestly reduce HbA1c and fasting blood glucose while improving quality of life and reducing diabetes-related distress.
