An active lifestyle and regular exercise, along with healthy eating, is the safest and most effective way to lose weight and keep it off. Even modest weight loss improves health. The main goal of obesity treatment is to adopt new, healthy ways of eating and make them part of a daily routine. Work with a provider and dietitian to set realistic, safe daily calorie targets that support weight loss while maintaining adequate nutrition.
Slow and steady weight loss is more sustainable than rapid weight loss. Losing 0.5 to 1 pound per week by reducing calorie intake by 500 calories per day is a commonly recommended approach. Extreme diets with fewer than 1,100 calories per day are not considered safe and often do not provide enough vitamins and minerals. Most people who lose weight through very low calorie diets return to overeating and develop weight regain.
Physical activity plays a critical role in both weight loss and weight maintenance. The goal for weight loss is at least 150 to 300 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity such as brisk walking, cycling, or swimming. For weight maintenance after loss, 200 to 300 minutes per week may be needed. Exercise should be increased gradually to reduce injury risk, and strength training should be included at least 2 days per week to preserve and build muscle mass during weight loss.
For successful weight management, reducing consumption of calorie-dense, low-nutrient foods is essential. This includes sugary beverages, fast food, processed snacks, sweets, and alcohol. These foods provide large numbers of calories with minimal satiety or nutritional value. Replacing them with high-volume, low-calorie foods such as non-starchy vegetables, fruits, legumes, and whole grains allows people to eat satisfying amounts of food while maintaining a calorie deficit.
Waist circumference is an important measure of obesity-related health risk beyond body weight alone. Excess abdominal fat is associated with increased risk of type 2 diabetes, heart disease, and stroke. A waist circumference greater than 35 inches for women or 40 inches for men indicates increased metabolic risk. Reducing abdominal fat through a combination of aerobic exercise, resistance training, and dietary changes improves metabolic health markers including blood glucose, blood pressure, and triglycerides.
Behavioral strategies are a critical component of sustainable weight management. These include self-monitoring of food intake and physical activity through journals or apps, setting specific and realistic goals, identifying and addressing emotional eating triggers, building social support, and planning ahead for challenging situations such as eating out or holiday meals. Counseling or structured support groups can provide accountability and help address the psychological factors that contribute to weight gain and difficulty losing weight.
Sleep quality and duration significantly affect weight and metabolic health. Getting fewer than 7 hours of sleep per night is associated with increased hunger and appetite, particularly for high-calorie foods, due to alterations in the hunger hormones leptin and ghrelin. Chronic sleep deprivation also impairs insulin sensitivity and increases cortisol levels, promoting fat storage especially in the abdominal area. Prioritizing 7 to 9 hours of quality sleep per night supports both weight management and overall metabolic health.
Stress management is an important component of obesity treatment. Chronic psychological stress elevates cortisol, which promotes abdominal fat accumulation, increases appetite for high-calorie comfort foods, and disrupts sleep. Effective stress management strategies for people with obesity include regular physical activity, mindfulness practices, adequate social support, and when needed, counseling or therapy to address underlying anxiety, depression, or emotional eating patterns.
