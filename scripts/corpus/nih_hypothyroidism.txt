Hypothyroidism, or underactive thyroid, happens when the thyroid gland does not make enough thyroid hormones to meet the body's needs. The thyroid is a small, butterfly-shaped gland in the front of the neck that makes hormones controlling the way the body uses energy. These hormones affect nearly every organ and control many of the body's most important functions, including breathing, heart rate, weight, digestion, and mood. Without enough thyroid hormones, many bodily functions slow down.
The primary treatment for hypothyroidism is thyroid hormone replacement therapy with levothyroxine, a synthetic form of the thyroid hormone T4. Levothyroxine is taken orally once daily, usually in the morning on an empty stomach. It should be taken consistently at the same time each day. For the medication to be absorbed properly, it should not be taken within 4 hours of calcium supplements, iron supplements, or antacids, as these can interfere with absorption. Most people with hypothyroidism require lifelong hormone replacement therapy.
Hypothyroidism can contribute to elevated LDL cholesterol and high total cholesterol, because thyroid hormones play a role in cholesterol metabolism. Treating hypothyroidism with levothyroxine often improves cholesterol levels. However, people with hypothyroidism who also have elevated cholesterol may still benefit from a heart-healthy diet low in saturated fat, rich in soluble fiber, and emphasizing fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and lean protein.
Weight gain is a common symptom of hypothyroidism because reduced thyroid hormone levels slow metabolism. While thyroid hormone replacement therapy typically leads to modest weight loss, many people do not return to their pre-hypothyroidism weight through medication alone. Sustainable weight management in hypothyroidism requires a combination of adequate hormone replacement, a balanced diet with appropriate caloric intake, and regular physical activity.
Fatigue is one of the most common and debilitating symptoms of hypothyroidism and may persist even after thyroid hormone levels normalize with medication. Regular moderate exercise — including aerobic activities such as walking, cycling, or swimming, and resistance training — can help combat fatigue by improving cardiovascular fitness, muscle strength, and overall energy levels. Starting with low to moderate intensity and gradually increasing duration and frequency is recommended, especially for people who have been sedentary.
Constipation is a frequent symptom of hypothyroidism because reduced thyroid hormones slow gastrointestinal motility. Dietary strategies to manage constipation include increasing fiber intake through whole grains, fruits, vegetables, and legumes; drinking adequate water (at least 8 cups per day); and engaging in regular physical activity, which stimulates bowel motility. These approaches support digestive health while also providing broader cardiovascular and metabolic benefits.
Iodine is an essential mineral for thyroid hormone production. However, for people already diagnosed with hypothyroidism who are on thyroid hormone replacement therapy, excessive iodine supplementation is not recommended and can actually worsen thyroid function in some individuals. Most people in the United States get adequate iodine from iodized salt and dairy products. A balanced diet generally provides sufficient iodine without the need for supplementation.
