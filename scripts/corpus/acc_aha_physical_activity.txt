For substantial health benefits, adults should do at least 150 to 300 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic physical activity, or 75 to 150 minutes per week of vigorous-intensity aerobic activity, or an equivalent combination of both. Aerobic activity should preferably be spread throughout the week rather than concentrated in one or two days. Adults who sit less and do any amount of moderate-to-vigorous physical activity gain some health benefit — some activity is always better than none.
Adults should also perform muscle-strengthening activities of moderate or greater intensity involving all major muscle groups on 2 or more days per week. Major muscle groups include the legs, hips, back, abdomen, chest, shoulders, and arms. Muscle-strengthening activities include lifting weights, working with resistance bands, doing exercises that use body weight for resistance such as push-ups and squats, and some forms of yoga. These activities provide health benefits beyond what aerobic exercise alone provides.
Older adults should incorporate multicomponent physical activity that includes balance training as well as aerobic and muscle-strengthening activities. Balance training reduces fall risk, which is a major cause of injury and disability in older adults. Examples of balance training include standing on one foot, walking heel to toe, and activities like tai chi. The key guidelines for adults also apply to older adults, adjusted for individual fitness level and any chronic conditions present.
Seven of the ten most common chronic diseases in American adults are favorably influenced by regular physical activity. These include heart disease, type 2 diabetes, high blood pressure, obesity, depression, and certain cancers. Regular physical activity is linked to reduced risk of premature mortality, cardiovascular disease events, and disease progression. Physical activity benefits extend to people of all ages, sexes, races, ethnicities, and fitness levels, including those living with chronic conditions.
Adults with chronic conditions or disabilities who are able to do so should meet the key guidelines of 150 to 300 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity and 2 or more days per week of muscle-strengthening activity. When chronic conditions make it impossible to meet these targets, individuals should be as physically active as their abilities and conditions allow. A physical therapist or certified exercise physiologist can help design safe, effective activity programs for individuals with significant physical limitations.
Health benefits from physical activity start accumulating immediately after a session of exercise, and even short episodes of physical activity contribute to overall health. The requirement that physical activity occur in continuous bouts of at least 10 minutes has been eliminated in current guidelines. Any amount of moderate-to-vigorous activity throughout the day counts toward weekly totals. This means that three 10-minute walks provide the same benefit as one 30-minute walk.
Regular physical activity produces significant improvements in brain health including reduced risk of depression and anxiety, improved cognitive function, and better sleep quality. People who are regularly physically active report feeling better, thinking more clearly, and sleeping better. These mental health benefits are important for people managing chronic conditions where depression and anxiety are common comorbidities, as well as for the general population seeking improved quality of life.
Sedentary behavior — time spent sitting or lying down while awake — is associated with increased risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and premature mortality, independent of the total amount of physical activity a person does. Moving more throughout the day and sitting less is recommended for all adults. Simple strategies to reduce sedentary time include standing or walking during phone calls, taking the stairs, parking farther away, and taking brief walking breaks during prolonged desk work.
