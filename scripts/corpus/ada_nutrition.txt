Reducing overall carbohydrate intake for individuals with diabetes has demonstrated the most evidence for improving glycemia and may be applied in a variety of eating patterns. For individuals with type 2 diabetes not meeting glycemic targets, reducing carbohydrate intake to below 130g per day is supported as a first-line dietary approach. Foods with a low glycemic index — including non-starchy vegetables, legumes, and whole grains — should be emphasized over refined carbohydrates, added sugars, and sugar-sweetened beverages.
Eating patterns that have been shown to be effective for people with type 2 diabetes include Mediterranean-style, low-carbohydrate, and plant-based diets. No single eating pattern is superior for all individuals. The most important factor in dietary adherence is individual preference and sustainability. A registered dietitian nutritionist with expertise in diabetes management can help tailor a meal plan to an individual's metabolic goals, food preferences, and cultural traditions.
For people with diabetes, the Diabetes Plate method offers a simple, visual framework: half the plate should be non-starchy vegetables such as broccoli, spinach, tomatoes, and peppers; one quarter should be lean protein such as chicken, fish, eggs, or tofu; and the remaining quarter should be carbohydrate-containing foods such as whole grains, legumes, or starchy vegetables. This approach supports portion control and glycemic management without requiring calorie counting.
Sodium intake should be limited to less than 2,300 mg per day for most individuals with diabetes. Processed foods, canned soups, deli meats, and restaurant meals are the primary sources of excess sodium in the American diet. Reducing sodium intake is particularly important for individuals with diabetes who also have hypertension, as both conditions significantly elevate cardiovascular risk.
Dietary fat quality matters more than quantity for people with diabetes. Replacing saturated fats (found in red meat, full-fat dairy, and tropical oils) with unsaturated fats (found in olive oil, nuts, seeds, and fatty fish) is associated with improved LDL cholesterol and reduced cardiovascular risk. Trans fats should be avoided entirely. Omega-3 fatty acids found in fatty fish such as salmon, sardines, and mackerel have anti-inflammatory effects and support cardiovascular health.
Fiber intake should be prioritized in the diets of people with diabetes. Dietary fiber slows glucose absorption, reduces postprandial blood glucose spikes, improves LDL cholesterol, and supports gut health. Adults should aim for at least 25 to 38 grams of dietary fiber per day, primarily from food sources including legumes, whole grains, vegetables, and fruits rather than supplements.
Sugar-sweetened beverages including sodas, fruit juices, energy drinks, and sweetened coffees and teas should be minimized or eliminated in people with diabetes. These beverages deliver rapidly absorbed carbohydrates that cause sharp blood glucose elevations without providing satiety or nutritional benefit. Water, unsweetened coffee, unsweetened tea, and sparkling water are preferred alternatives.
Meal timing and consistency can improve glycemic control in people with diabetes. Eating at regular intervals, avoiding prolonged fasting followed by large meals, and distributing carbohydrate intake evenly across meals and snacks helps prevent large blood glucose swings. Skipping breakfast in particular has been associated with higher postprandial blood glucose levels at subsequent meals.
